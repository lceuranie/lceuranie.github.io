SBSP — global roadmaps and the divergence of intents.

Why incompatible national success metrics — not technology readiness — are the real bottleneck to global collaboration on Space-Based Solar Power.

Date
Submitted January 2026
Role
Sole author — individual MSS research report
Length
~4,000 words (abstract to conclusion)
Method
Architectural phylogenetic review · comparative roadmap analysis
Subject
Space-Based Solar Power Systems (SBSP)

01The argument

Space-Based Solar Power has been proposed, redrawn, and redesigned for sixty years. The standard explanation for why it is still not built is that the technology is not ready — launch costs are too high, in-orbit assembly is too immature, microwave power transmission has not been demonstrated at scale. This report argues that this explanation is wrong.

The technology is not the bottleneck. The bottleneck is the divergence of intents — three major national programmes (the United States, Europe and the United Kingdom, China) measure success against incompatible metrics, and that incompatibility forecloses the global collaboration that SBSP would otherwise require to be economically viable. The report develops this argument through two methodological halves: an architectural phylogeny and a comparative roadmap analysis.

02A phylogenetic review of SBSP architectures

Borrowing the cladistic approach from evolutionary biology, the report treats SBSP architectures as taxa and reconstructs their inheritance tree across five orders of design.

Monolithic era (1970s–1990s)

Glaser's original satellite, the 1979 SPS reference system. Single large structures, microwave transmission, GEO. The architectural lineage all subsequent proposals work to escape from or refine.

Branch order 1 — Gravity-gradient structures (1990s–2000s)

Reductions in mass and assembly burden by using gravity gradient stabilisation rather than active control across the entire structure. SunTower-class proposals.

Branch order 2 — Biomimetic structures (2010s)

SPS-ALPHA and similar architectures that decompose the satellite into self-similar replicated modules — taking inspiration from biological tissue rather than industrial manufacturing.

Branch order 3 — Solid-state structures (2020s)

The Caltech SSPP / MAPLE family. Phased-array, planar tile architectures with no mechanical pointing of individual elements; lower mass, simpler assembly, but harder thermal and beam-control problems.

Branch order 4 — Optical structures

Laser-based wireless power transmission as a complement or alternative to microwave. Higher beam intensity per area, narrower link budget tolerance, different regulatory profile — and a different engineering temperament from the microwave lineage that came before.

The phylogenetic tree makes an empirical point that S-curves and TRL ladders cannot: the design space is mature. There are several distinct viable architectural lineages, not a single race-to-readiness. That maturity is what reframes the bottleneck.

03The "Big Three" roadmaps

The report compares three national programmes against the success metric each implicitly optimises for.

United States — the sceptical economic approach

The dominant US metric is Levelised Cost of Energy (LCOE). Against that yardstick, SBSP loses to terrestrial photovoltaics by an order of magnitude, and the policy stance is correspondingly cautious — research, not deployment.

Europe and the UK — net zero and decarbonisation

The European framing optimises for decarbonisation and energy sovereignty rather than headline cost. SBSP becomes attractive when measured against Value-Adjusted LCOE (VALCOE), which prices in dispatchability and capacity contribution to the grid — exactly the dimensions where intermittent terrestrial renewables underperform.

China — first-mover advantage

The Chinese programme is structured around capability demonstration and the strategic positioning that comes with it. Success is being first; the cost benchmark is secondary to the geopolitical option-value of having the architecture in orbit before anyone else.

04The geopolitical super-bottleneck

The three success metrics are not just different — they are incompatible in the way that prevents joint funding, joint architecture decisions, and joint regulatory progress. A US partner optimising for LCOE will block design decisions that a European partner optimising for VALCOE would consider obvious; a Chinese programme optimising for first-mover advantage will not wait for either. The report calls this the "geopolitical super-bottleneck" and argues it dominates the technological bottlenecks the standard analysis emphasises.

The other foreseeable bottlenecks the report works through — launch logistics and cadence, regulatory and spectrum allocation, orbital sustainability and debris, environmental and societal impacts — are real but secondary. They become tractable inside a single national programme; they become intractable across an unaligned consortium.

05Recommendations

The report's recommendations are pragmatic. Stop framing the SBSP problem as "we need a technology breakthrough" and start framing it as "we need a metric framework that lets at least two of the three major programmes co-invest in the same architecture." Concretely, this means working VALCOE and capacity-contribution accounting into the international policy conversation explicitly, and accepting that a fully global SBSP collaboration is unlikely in the current geopolitical environment — a successful first deployment will be regional, on the way to becoming a model that others can join later.

06Status

The report was submitted to the MSS programme in January 2026. A version of this work was prepared for submission to IAC 2026; the IAC review panel did not select the paper for this year's track, which on reflection is a fair call — the policy framing belongs in a longer-form policy venue more than in the IAC engineering tracks. The work is being reformulated accordingly.

07What this work demonstrates

SBSP is the project on this site that lives furthest from immediate engineering. That is intentional. Among the work I have done at ISU, this is the piece that exercises the systems-level technology-forecasting and global policy-roadmapping muscles — the same muscles that programme-management roles at agencies and primes look for. The mission-engineering work elsewhere on this site shows what I can build; this report shows how I think about what should be built, and why.
