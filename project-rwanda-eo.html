Vegetation health for Rwanda, in the browser.

A multi-temporal Sentinel-2 workflow with CNN-based classification, deployed as an interactive Google Earth Engine app for vegetation monitoring and change detection over a five-year window.

01Why Rwanda

Smallholder agriculture in East Africa is among the populations most exposed to climate-driven shocks, and among the least served by dedicated remote-sensing infrastructure. Rwanda is a useful study area: its terraced topography produces complex vegetation patterns at the scale Sentinel-2 resolves well, and the country's open data culture (RNRA, MINAGRI) gives ground-truth context for validation.

The goal of this work was modest but operational: produce a free, browser-accessible tool that lets a non-specialist visualise vegetation health and change over the last five years, anywhere in Rwanda, at any zoom level — without writing code, downloading imagery, or running their own classifier.

02Workflow

Data preparation

Sentinel-2 L2A surface reflectance, filtered for cloud cover under 20% per scene, mosaicked at quarterly cadence over the 2021–2026 window. Cloud-shadow masking via the SCL band, with additional pixel-level masking using the QA60 cirrus flags. Spectral indices computed: NDVI, EVI, NDWI, NBR.

Classification

A small CNN classifier trained on patch-level samples drawn from the Copernicus Global Land Cover product, fine-tuned on Rwanda-specific reference points. Five output classes: dense forest, agricultural mosaic, grassland, built-up, water. Class boundaries deliberately coarse — the goal is robust signal, not fine taxonomy.

Change detection

EVI delta computed between season-matched composites (e.g. dry-season 2021 vs dry-season 2026) to suppress phenology-driven false positives. Output is a per-pixel signed change product, served as a tile layer.

Deployment

The full workflow runs server-side in Google Earth Engine; the user-facing app is a GEE-hosted UI that exposes AOI, date range, and product-layer controls. No backend, no install, no GIS expertise required.

03Stakeholder framing

The conceptual stakeholder map for this work spans national agencies (MINAGRI, RNRA), international organisations (UN-SPIDER, FAO, WFP), and downstream operators in food security and disaster response. The deliberate design choice — public, browser-based, no licence — reflects what this user community actually needs: low-friction access far more than peak accuracy.

The work aligns with UN Sustainable Development Goals 2 (Zero Hunger) and 13 (Climate Action), and it sits in the lineage of broader Copernicus Africa initiatives that are increasingly important to EU space-policy positioning.

04Results

Validation against the ESA WorldCover 2021 reference at randomly sampled points yielded an overall classification accuracy of 84%, with the largest confusions falling along agricultural-mosaic / grassland boundaries — expected, given the coarseness of the class definition. The change product successfully recovered known reforestation projects (Gishwati-Mukura) and known clearance events along the eastern lowlands.

06Limitations & next steps

The model is deliberately simple — it favours recall over precision for vegetation gain, and it does not separate crop type. Two natural extensions: integrating Sentinel-1 SAR for cloud-resilient monitoring during the rainy season, and extending the workflow to the broader East African Community for regional reporting. A small set of fine-tuned class definitions (banana, tea, coffee — the three crops that dominate Rwanda's export economy) would also be a logical next step.
