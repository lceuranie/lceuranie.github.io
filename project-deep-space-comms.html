A roadmap for deep space communication.

Three phases. Three decades. A global plan to sustain communications for the missions humanity has not yet flown — accepted for presentation at IAC 2026.

01The problem

Deep space communication is the silent constraint on every ambitious mission concept. The Deep Space Network and ESTRACK are saturated, and the missions in serious planning — Mars Sample Return, the lunar gateway, the next generation of outer-planet probes, eventual crewed Mars — assume orders of magnitude more capacity than the current ground segment can supply. The question is not whether to expand: it is what to expand, where, on what timeline, and under what governance.

Our team's mandate, supported by a John Templeton Foundation grant and structured as the cornerstone team project of the ISU MSS 2026, was to produce a roadmap that takes that question seriously — across technologies (RF, optical, quantum), across geographies (the southern-hemisphere gap), and across institutions.

02What I contributed

Within the 30-plus-person team, I worked in the roadmap working group, focused on phasing and governance. My specific contributions: structuring the three-phase delivery plan (near-term capacity expansion, mid-term optical and inter-satellite links, long-term quantum-and-beyond architectures), drafting the governance section that maps which phases plausibly sit with which institutional actors (national agencies, ESA, commercial primes, multilateral consortia), and contributing to the risk/dependency analysis that determines which capabilities must precede which others.

03Three phases

Phase 1 — Capacity (near-term)

Expand the existing RF ground network with new antennas in the southern hemisphere, automate scheduling, and adopt next-generation coding to extract more bits per second from existing infrastructure. This is the cheapest, most certain phase, and it buys the time the next two phases need.

Phase 2 — Optical & inter-satellite (mid-term)

Operationalise optical links — both ground-to-space at high data rates and inter-satellite for relay topology in cislunar and beyond. The challenges here are atmospheric, regulatory, and architectural: who owns the relays, who pays, who has access?

Phase 3 — Quantum and beyond (long-term)

Position for quantum communication primitives — entanglement-based key distribution, quantum-limited receivers — that change the security and capacity envelope of deep space links. This phase is necessarily speculative; the roadmap's job is to keep it on the agenda without overcommitting near-term resources to it.

04Governance

The roadmap distinguishes between technology readiness and institutional readiness. A capability that is technically mature can still be blocked by absent regulation, missing standards, or unresolved spectrum allocation. The governance section maps each phase to the institutional actors plausibly responsible for it, and identifies the standards bodies (CCSDS, ITU-R) and policy venues (UNCOPUOS) where the missing pieces must be negotiated.

05IAC 2026

The team's paper based on this work was accepted for presentation at the International Astronautical Congress 2026. Authorship and exact title are managed at the team level; the project is one of two ISU MSS 2026 team projects accepted by the IAC review panel this year.

06Why this work matters to me

The roadmap exercise is, in essence, a programme-management problem at planetary scale: dependencies, phasing, risk, governance. Every instinct I built up running ten years of multi-stakeholder industrial projects translates here directly — the substance is different, the discipline is the same. It is the most convincing argument I can make that the project-management half of my background and the systems-engineering half of my new training are not two careers but one.
