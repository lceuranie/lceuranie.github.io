01The work

From 2020 to 2024 I led multidisciplinary engineering teams of five-to-ten staff through low-to-medium complexity geosciences project life cycles, with portfolio revenues per project in the $50K–$250K range. In parallel, I directed a multi-phase, large-scale information-system digital transformation across SLB's European operations — a programme that improved data integrity by 50% and rebuilt the workflows that the geosciences delivery teams relied on.

The combination of run-the-business project delivery and change-the-business transformation is the part of this experience I lean on most when I describe what programme management means in practice. They reinforce each other: the transformation programme had no value if the run-the-business projects faltered, and the run-the-business projects could not improve their delivery without the transformation.

02Why this maps onto ESA-style governance

Programme management at this scale is documentation discipline at every step. Each of the bullets below was the daily habit, not the rare deliverable.

Cross-functional coordination

I coordinated cross-functional teams — including support functions (finance, IT, engineering, commercial) — to deliver more than 10 technical projects, managing scope, schedule, risks, and stakeholder communication. In substance this is what ESA calls internal work-package coordination: the same activity, same artefacts, different vocabulary.

Structured documentation for decision-making

Process maps, risk logs, variance reports, dashboards — produced not as box-ticking but as the substrate for steering-committee decisions. The ESA programme-review expectation that every claim sits behind a documented artefact is the same expectation I worked under at SLB.

Schedule and KPI discipline

I developed and maintained schedule and KPI dashboards in MS Project and Power BI, achieving 100% on-time release of project deliverables across the portfolio. The dashboards were not pretty pictures — they were the operational tool the team and leadership used every week.

Governance, variance, and change-request workflows

I monitored governance, variances, and progress tracking against performance metrics within quality standards; executed change-request workflows; and maintained alignment with contractual and compliance requirements throughout the project lifecycle.

Risk analytics across operations

I developed a risk analytics dashboard that aggregated across more than 50 operations, which became the team's accountability and awareness instrument. The point was less the dashboard than the cultural change: making risk a first-class object in everyone's week.

Audit, lessons-learned, close-out, invoice

I oversaw 10+ audits, documented 50+ lessons-learned, ran close-out and handover for projects through their full lifecycle, and delivered on-time invoice and collection. The boring discipline at the end of a project is where most of the cost-overrun risk hides; my record on this side of the work is part of why the transformation programme succeeded.
