A federal architecture for European space defense.

Designing the European Space Defense Administration: a federal governance framework, three strategic pillars, and a five-year, €15 billion annual roadmap to consolidate fragmented national space assets into a unified command structure.

01The brief

The workshop scenario placed the team in a hypothetical 2026 in which the European Union has become a federal state. The European Federal Government — formerly the European Commission — has commissioned a proposal for the structure of a new European Space Defense Administration (ESDA): governance, organisational chart, three strategic focus areas, and a five-year budget. Our deliverables were a 2,000-word strategy note and a seven-minute presentation to the faculty panel acting as the President's cabinet.

The proposal had to grapple with a problem that real European institutions face: orbital awareness and space-defense capability today are a "jigsaw puzzle" of national sensors and bilateral agreements, fragmented across member states with overlapping mandates and gaps that adversaries can exploit. The ESDA's purpose is to convert that jigsaw into a single federal entity without paralysing the national capabilities that already exist.

02Three strategic pillars

Pillar 1 — Integrated Space Domain Awareness

A unified, real-time federal eye in the sky to eliminate blind spots. The pillar consolidates national SDA sensors and adds a "Guardian" constellation to achieve 100% threat attribution — the capacity to characterise the intent of any approaching object, not just observe it.

Pillar 2 — The European Space Shield (Active Resilience)

Centralised capability to protect federal assets and respond to electronic warfare (jamming, spoofing) and physical kinetic threats. The pillar treats space as a contested domain rather than a sanctuary, and budgets for hardening — €5 billion is allocated explicitly to the 24/7 maintenance and active hardening of ground segments, on the principle that an unhardened ground network makes the orbital architecture above it irrelevant.

Pillar 3 — EU Traffic Sovereignty (Responsive Space)

The capacity to replace lost assets in days rather than years. The pillar targets a 72-hour responsive launch capability, sovereign access via Ariane 6, and a federal logistics chain that can absorb the loss of a satellite without losing the service it provides.

03The "Delta" command structure

A federation of 27 nations cannot pause to debate when a satellite is jammed. The proposal adopts a unified High Command with a binding authority for time-critical decisions, supported by four directorates and the US Space Force–inspired "Delta" concept: small, single-purpose units led by a commander accountable for one specific job. Delta 2 does Space Domain Awareness; Delta 3 does Electronic Warfare; Delta Y is the orbital warfare unit; Delta 10 handles Dual-Use Synergy explicitly because Europe — unlike the US or China — must integrate civilian and military infrastructure rather than separate them.

The directorates are deliberately built on a distributed-leadership model (Denis, Langley & Sergi, 2012) rather than a single hierarchical chain, with liaison officers between Operations and Systems to break the procurement-to-deployment lag that bedevils current European defense procurement.

04Civil-military synergy

The structural choice that most distinguishes the ESDA proposal from the US or Chinese model is the explicit Civil-Military Directorate. Where the US Space Force and the People's Liberation Army Strategic Support Force keep these wings separate, the ESDA integrates them — which means Galileo PNT, Copernicus EO, and the future IRIS² constellation are designed and operated to serve both border security and civilian disaster response from day one. Maximising federal efficiency, but also reflecting a genuinely European policy preference for dual-use infrastructure over parallel military and civilian assets.

05The €15 billion roadmap

The five-year budget (2026–2031) supports the three pillars at roughly €15 billion per year, with the largest single line being the €5 billion ground-segment maintenance and hardening allocation. The proposal aligns with the ESA Zero Debris 2030 target and the existing EU Space Strategy for Security and Defense — not as an alternative but as the federal-state evolution of those policies.

06What I contributed

Within the six-person team, I worked across the governance, roadmap, and presentation threads. On the analytical side: contributing to the federal organisational structure, the rationale for an explicit Civil-Military directorate as a European-distinctive choice, and the budget breakdown across the three pillars. On the delivery side: I was one of the two team members who presented the proposal in the seven-minute live presentation to the faculty panel acting as the President's cabinet, fielding questions on the dual-use directorate and the budget allocation choices.

The work synthesised material from the EU Space Strategy for Security and Defense, the ESA Zero Debris policy, US Space Force public doctrine, and academic literature on distributed leadership in turbulent governance contexts.

07Why this work matters to me

European space policy has been moving steadily toward a more explicit security and defense posture — IRIS², the EU Space Strategy for Security and Defense, the increased budget for security applications under Horizon Europe and the Defense Fund. After my ESA internship I would like to contribute to this domain, and the ESDA workshop is the most direct evidence on this site of substantive engagement with it.

The work also draws on instincts from a decade of multi-stakeholder programme management: the central problems in space defense governance — fragmented authority, overlapping mandates, slow procurement, contested change-control — are exactly the problems that programme-management discipline is designed to solve, scaled up.
